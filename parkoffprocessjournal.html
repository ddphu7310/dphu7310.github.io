Week 1

This week, nothing much really happened apart from forming groups and the briefing of our assessments. I have joined groups with Edmond Hua and Patrick Hwang. Already working with them in previous semesters, I believe we can work well together as a group of 3.

Once our assessments were explained, as a way to tackle the design brief of how we can create an interface for autonomy, we used a method called Brain-Dumping. Brain-Dumping essentially is a method to fixate on the larger problem through minor ideas. Jotting down 2 relevant problems and 2 out of this world problems, they are then accordingly arranged and compiled between group members. The main reason for this brainstorming method is to analyse between the extreme contrast between two conflicting ideas, potentially bringing to light specific and universal problems.

This is the result of our Brain-Dumping session. Looking at the bigger picture, it is clear that both sides contain problems more focused on cars and a few with other vehicles.

Week 2

In our week 2 tutorial, we learned more about the design process where we looked deeper into each step. The steps for a standard design process is:

  • Define the Problem Space
  • Do Background Research
  • Specify the actual problem to solve
  • Brainstorm solutions
  • Choose the best solution
  • Do detailed design & development work to define the concept
  • Build a prototype
  • Test and redesign

Obviously we would be focusing on defining the problem space as we are so early into the semester and our concept.

The tutorial also showed us a new way of coming up and targeting on ideas which was the stakeholder map. A stakeholder map is used to visually consolidate and communicate the key components to a design project.

Ultimately through this map, we can clearly see that cars have a major influence in daily human interactions.

So with these key problem defining methods, we thoroughly discussed what autonomous interaction would be the driving force in our design process and final concept.

In the end we chose to focus on car parking. Parking is virtually essential to all vehicle owners. Every journey involves parking at the destination and in-between. With car ownership per household rising exponentially per year, conflicts in parking are acknowledged. These conflicts are largely defined in terms of supply (too few spaces are available, somebody must build more) or in terms of management (available facilities are used efficiently and should be better managed).

User Interviews

Interviews are used to build up a deep understanding of the individual’s life, decisions and challenges. Interviews are “structured conversations” where questions are planned beforehand. 10 informal interviews were conducted, where all of them were university undergraduates within in the age 18-21. All interviewees are general drivers that commute to and from employment, education and recreation.

Interview Insights:

  • All interviewees commented about the lack of parking.
  • The extreme parking rates in certain suburbs and parking lots.
  • Navigation (signage) within parking lots were confusing.

User Surveys

An online survey was created where we aim to include the main themes discussed in the previous interviews, and general demographics of drivers. The survey was shared and sent out to large university groups with the assumption that the majority of members are able to drive.

Questions included in the survey were:

  • Where do you usually park?
  • On average, how long does it take for you to find a parking spot?
  • How long do you usually keep your car parked for?
  • What are some problems or frustrations that you have experienced regarding Parking?
  • What are some ways you can solve these problems?
  • How do you think Automation (Self-Driving Cars, Automated Carparks, etc) can improve the Parking experience?

Survey Insights

  • Majority of participants are aged between 18-25 and are currently studying full-time as a student. 3 most popular and general modes of transportation are by car, train and bus, using these 3-7 days a week.
  • “Where do you usually drive to?” Of our 49 participants, popular responses include for the purpose of recreation, errands and for employment. University being in the lower end of the responses with 35%, it is easily justified due to the high cost rates in university parking as well as the supply of parking spaces available.
  • In addition to the data asking where participants usually park, street parking is more favoured possibly due to ease of access, flexible time limits and parking rates.

The research report consists of researching existing products that utilise autonomy in car parking. Briefly summarised, there are two products that largely utilise autonomous parking is the Ford Collaborative Parking and the Automated Parking System (APS).

Ford Collaborative

Ford’s Collaborative Parking technology compiles data from sensors belonging to vehicles in a certain carpark. This data combined with the parking lots monitoring system to determine a parking spot it vacant. Collaborative parking is possibly able to coincide with autonomous driving where cars can be able to use this data to accurately navigate through a parking lot safely when arriving to the vacant spot.

Automated Parking System (APS)

A multi-tiered car parking system that essentially parks cars in specialised crates, stacking them on top of each other on several levels. This method allows for 20 cars to be parked in the same footprint of 4 parking spots. The utilisation of computer systems, sensors and cameras, require little effort for the drivers as the APS moves through the garage, finding an empty spot rather than the driver finding it themselves, ultimately reducing time and fuel wastage. However, this is a costly method as mechanical components need to be produced and a specific area needs to be developed. Currently the largest APS in Europa is in Denmark, providing 1000 parking spots via 20 lifts.

After conducting user research we came back together and began to synthesize our insights and brainstorm solutions to the main problems we identified. We began to develop these insights and concepts with storyboards to visualise the best scenario for our respective ideas.

Idea 1 - Phone Controlled Automated Parking App

Connecting this mobile app to an autonomous car, they are able to allocate navigation as well as initiating autonomous parking. The app virtually controls parking all within one app where it will pay for parking fines, pay overtime parking rates, select the users desired vacant parking spot and to also autonomously navigate their car to their destination. This phone app aims to eliminate the need for autonomous car owners to worry about the availability of parking in their area or the availability of parking at their destination.

Idea 2 - In-Car Interface

This concept is an In-car User Interface used to self-park the car after the passenger alights. The interaction occurs through the car’s inbuilt information systems screen. Once the car is almost arrived at the drop-off destination, it will queue the user to select parking preferences depending on Price, Time and Geography. The left side of the interface provides options, whereas the right side provides live information such as current location and nearby parking options.

Idea 3 - HUD AI Teaching

Based off the user research results from the first assessment, we discovered that there were many individuals that felt that the problem with parking and congestion was due to the overall lack of driving skill in drivers. HUD is an AI Teaching system that is integrated into the users car, it teaches the user where and how to drive, with AI driving assist features. It uses a Augmented Reality display integrated into the cars screen. The concept is a two step process consisting of the phone app and the AR screen. The user selects their parking destination and is provided with information about the parking availability, then uses Near Field Communication (NFI) in the car to send the destination to the car - making the process as seamless as possible.


After finishing our storyboards, we started sketching up concepts through a series of annotated hand-drawn UI sketches depicting potential solutions for the visual interface. These sketches include the most important screens rather than the minute details such as login and settings screens.

Week 8 + Mid-Semester Break

Heuristic Evaluation was conducted to identify any specific problems, and assess the usability of our interfaces by comparing it Nielsen’s 10 Principles for Interaction Design, also known as “heuristics”.

We identified any problems encountered in Think-alouds or discovered by asking the user post-experience. For each of the problems, we identified which of Nielsen’s Heuristics it violated. Each group member would assess the severity from 1 - 5, with 1 having no issue, and 5 being catastrophic. All the scores were aggregated into an average. Users were also asked for any feedback or recommendations for each problem identified. We decided to use this user evaluation method as it is a useful method that requires low cost and can be conducted in a short time span. This method was also complementary to conducting think alouds, and identifying correct Heuristics is valuable for iterative and correct design.

We we will also be using think-aloud user testing in conjunction with Heuristics evaluation as key problems from think-aloud will be further evaluated with Heuristics. We chose this Think-Aloud user testing Method as it helped us understand participants’ real-time thoughts and emotions as they occur, and as they attempt to work through issues they encounter. We decided to test with a single scenario for each concept, as the paper prototypes were still low-fidelity and had a linear user journey.


We also used First Click Testing which involves observing our users interactions to see the percentage of what users would interact with first and so on. First Click Test allows us to evaluate and understand the effectiveness and functionality of our wireframes, also giving us insights about how users differently approach their given tasks. This evaluation was done by timing and tracking each of the users clicks in relation to a set of given tasks. Users were then asked to rate the difficulty of each task from 1 - 5. 1 being easy and 5 being difficulty.

So with the evaluation methods decided, we spent the rest of the week evaluating our ideas with these three methods.

Now with all the data collected, we need to analyse the data in order to approach our finalised concept appropriately.

Some of these included:

  • Flipping the settings button and menu to the left hand side to be more in line with common design practice.
  • Pop-up windows for error prevention

Process Reflection

We chose the user evaluation methods that we used because they:

  • Were rapid and low cost.
  • Helped us understand users’ mental models
  • Identified key issues in the UI.

These things were what allowed us to rapidly iterate multiple times upon our chosen initial concept.
Possible ares of improvement:

  • Set multiple tasks for the think-aloud that segmentizes the user flow.
  • Recruit additional experts for heuristic evaluation.
  • Additional rapid user evaluation methods such as UMUX-lite (Mini version of SUS)
  • Organise and schedule user studies ahead of time.


Next Steps

As it gave us extremely valuable data, we will continue to utilise Think-aloud and Heuristic evaluation methods. However we will create more specific concrete tasks that will evaluate different aspects of the UI and user journey. We may also incorporate other rapid methods such as UMUX-lite for a more rounded evaluation. We want to stay lean, by conducting frequent, light user studies.
Going forward, we plan to iterate on our mid-fidelity wireframe using advanced prototyping tools such as Framer X and create a near-production hi-fidelity prototype that will encompass our entire user journey, such as payments, pickup and account settings.
